BIREFRINGENCE` SYMBOL             

Crystals Guide

This tutorial shows how to perform calculations of ray refraction and reflection at interfaces between homogeneous birefringent media.  Both uniaxial and biaxial crystal materials can be modeled as a birefringent media.

Polaris-M will automatically calculate ray splitting.  Rays will split upon reflection and refraction inside a birefringent media.

The Polarization Ray Tracing Matrix (PRT) will contain the polarizer properties to signal the polarization state in a given ray mode.  

To use a crystal material, a user must enter:
sys`material1`type→"Crystal"
    or
sys`material2`type→"Crystal"
In the CreateSurface function

All additional crystal inputs must use the material call out of the same numbered designation (either sys`material1 or sys`material2) as used in the type callout above.

The specific callout used (sys`material1 or sys`material2) does not effect the order of the materials during a raytrace.

Birefringent materials provide the prescription for describing uniaxial and biaxial materials in the optical system.

Addtional Inputs:
*Note that all addtional inputs also apply to sys`material2`_

sys`material1`crystal`name → “Crystal_Calcite” (Default)
    A textual identifier of catalog name (i.e. “Crystal_Calcite”) for material on one side of an interface. All inputs must be strings in the form of “Crystal_material”.
    
sys`material1`crystal`orientation → {{0, 0, 1.0}} (Default)
    If material is uniaxial, the optic axis is specified i.e {0,0,1}. For biaxial materials, the axis associated with high index of refraction is specified first, followed by the axis associated with the low index of refraction i.e. {{1,0,0},{0,1,0}}. Axes are specified in global coordinates.
    
sys`material1`crystal`orientGyro → {0,0,1} (Default)
    If material is uniaxial, the gyrotropic optic axis is specified i.e {0,0,1}. For biaxial materials, the axis associated with high gyrotropic constant is specified first, followed by the axis associated with the low gyrotropic constant i.e. {{1,0,0},{0,1,0}}. Axes are specified in global coordinates.

When the propagation vector is not parallel to the crystal axis, Polaris-M will automatically split rays into ordinary and extraordinary modes.  This causes two rays to hit the detector in different locations (as seen in the r column) for every input ray.  The difference in the optical path lengths for the two modes can be seen in the OPLCumlative column.  In this case the extraordinary ray (first row), has a slightly higher transmission.  This is because the component of the Mueller Matrix is slightly larger.

TIR in a Uniaxial Crystal (non-sequential)  (1)

This example will use a non-sequential ray trace.

surfaceLabel v a aperture material1 material2
Surface1 Crystal Entrance Face Abs[#1]<4&&botHight<#2<4& Air
Surface2 Crystal Exit Face Abs[#1]<4&&botHight<#2<4& Air
Surface3 Crystal Bottom Abs[#1]<4&&1<#3<10.& Air
Surface4 Detector Norm[{#1,#2}]<6.&&botHight<#2& Air Air

The ray splits into an ordinary and extraordianary mode.  The ordinary mode travels straight through the crystal.  The extraordinary ray deflects towards the bottom of the crystal and goes through TIR.  The TIR causes an additional ray splitting into extraordinary-ordinary modes and extraordinary-extraordinary modes.  Thus three rays are incident on the detector surface.

When the ray segments incident upon the detector are examined, we see three unique ray paths.  The ordinary ray has a PRT matrix that acts as a horizontal polarizer .  The output modes are shown below:

Biaxial Examples  (2)

Single Slab of KTP  (1)

The main difference between using uniaxial and biaxial crystals is the input for the parameter sys`material1`crystal`orientation.  In the uniaxial case this input was a 1 by 3 array describing the extraordinary axis, or crystal axis.  With biaxial crystal, the sys`material1`crystal`orientation input must be a 2 by 3 array.  The array must contain the axis orientation of the high index direction, followed by the low index direction.

With the crystal parameterized, it can be made into an optical system.

surfaceLabel shape v a material1 material2 aperture coating mode surfaceScatter
Surface1 Fronface of Crystal Air Norm[{#1,#2}]<crystalFaceSize& {}
Surface2 Backface of Crystal Air Norm[{#1,#2}]<crystalFaceSize& {}
Surface3 Detector Air Air Norm[{#1,#2}]<crystalFaceSize& Absorb {}

With the optical system defined, rays can be defined and then traced

Now, when the ray data is analyzed, we see that there is clear ray splitting of the high mode and the low mode.  Similarly, from the PRT and MM matrix data, we can see that the vertically polarized light (Y-Direction) couples into the high mode, and that horizontally polarized light couples into the low mode.
